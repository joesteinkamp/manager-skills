# Design Level Expectations

Reference for calibrating objectives to the report's level. Use this to ensure objectives are appropriately scoped.

## Level Framework

### IC1 — Junior Designer

**Scope:** Individual tasks and components within a project led by someone else.

**Delivery expectations:**
- Executes well-defined design tasks with guidance.
- Delivers screens and components that match design system standards.
- Meets deadlines with manager support on prioritization.

**Craft expectations:**
- Demonstrates growing proficiency in core design tools (Figma, prototyping).
- Designs for happy-path states consistently; beginning to consider error and empty states.
- Applies existing design system components correctly.

**Growth signals:**
- Asks good questions.
- Incorporates feedback quickly.
- Shows curiosity about user research and design rationale.
- Seeks out learning opportunities proactively.

**Objective scope:** Task-level. "Design the [component/screen] with [states]."

---

### IC2 — Designer

**Scope:** Complete features within a project, with some guidance on approach.

**Delivery expectations:**
- Owns feature-level design work from brief to handoff.
- Delivers complete specs with most states documented.
- Manages own timeline with occasional check-ins.

**Craft expectations:**
- Consistent visual execution across a feature.
- Documents all major states (default, error, loading, empty).
- Begins to consider responsive behavior and edge cases.

**Growth signals:**
- Participates in design critiques constructively.
- Begins to articulate design rationale beyond "it looks right."
- Shows initiative in exploring alternatives before converging.

**Objective scope:** Feature-level. "Design [feature] with full state coverage."

---

### IC3 — Mid-Level Designer

**Scope:** Full projects from discovery through delivery, with minimal guidance on approach.

**Delivery expectations:**
- Leads project-level design work end-to-end.
- Collaborates effectively with PM and eng.
- Delivers build-ready specs that eng can implement without significant clarification.

**Craft expectations:**
- Strong, consistent craft across all deliverables.
- Full state coverage is automatic (default, empty, loading, error, disabled).
- Considers responsive behavior, accessibility, and design system implications.
- Problem-frames before solution-exploring.

**Growth signals:**
- Gives useful feedback in design critiques.
- Communicates design rationale clearly to non-designers.
- Begins to influence project scope and direction.
- Self-identifies skill gaps and seeks development.

**Objective scope:** Project-level. "Lead design for [project] from discovery through spec."

---

### IC4 — Senior Designer

**Scope:** Multiple projects or a product area, with influence on what to work on.

**Delivery expectations:**
- Defines and drives design direction for a product area.
- Influences product roadmap through design insight.
- Delivers work that raises the quality bar for the team.
- Handles ambiguity — can take a vague problem and structure it.

**Craft expectations:**
- Craft is a baseline, not a differentiator — it's expected to be strong.
- Systems-level thinking: considers design system, platform, and cross-feature implications.
- Balances craft quality with shipping velocity deliberately.

**Growth signals:**
- Mentors junior and mid-level designers.
- Drives alignment across design, product, and engineering.
- Leads design reviews and critiques.
- Communicates confidently with senior stakeholders.
- Begins to shape team processes and standards.

**Objective scope:** Product-area-level. "Define and ship the design direction for [product area]."

---

### IC5 — Staff Designer

**Scope:** Cross-team or org-level design influence, with impact beyond their immediate team.

**Delivery expectations:**
- Sets design direction across multiple teams or a platform.
- Defines the "what" and "how" for design initiatives that span quarters.
- Unblocks other designers by providing frameworks, patterns, and strategic clarity.

**Craft expectations:**
- Craft decisions serve strategic goals, not just user-level quality.
- Defines design quality standards for others to follow.
- Contributes foundational patterns to the design system.

**Growth signals:**
- Influences product strategy at the leadership level.
- Mentors senior designers.
- Represents design in cross-functional leadership forums.
- Builds institutional knowledge (documentation, processes, playbooks).
- Is sought out for their judgment, not just their design output.

**Objective scope:** Org-level. "Establish the design vision for [platform/area] across [N] teams."

---

### IC6+ — Principal / Distinguished Designer

**Scope:** Company-wide design influence, with impact on the design discipline itself.

**Delivery expectations:**
- Shapes product and design strategy at the company level.
- Drives initiatives that affect the entire design org.
- Creates leverage — their work enables others to do better work.

**Craft expectations:**
- Defines what "great" looks like for the company.
- Balances design excellence with business realities at the strategic level.

**Growth signals:**
- Industry-level influence (talks, writing, open-source, standards bodies).
- Builds and evolves the design culture.
- Attracts talent through reputation.
- Identifies future opportunities before they're obvious.

**Objective scope:** Company-level. "Define the company's design strategy for [year/era]."

## Using This Reference

- Match objectives to the report's **current level**, not their aspirational level.
- For stretch goals, scope to **current level + 1** — never +2.
- If a report consistently demonstrates next-level behaviors, document it for promotion evidence — but don't set next-level objectives as baseline expectations until they're promoted.
- Use these levels to calibrate during objective-setting conversations: "At your level, here's what we'd expect. Here's where you're ahead, and here's where there's room to grow."
