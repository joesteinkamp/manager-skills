# Design Engineer Criteria

The criteria for evaluating a LinkedIn profile against a design engineer role.
Two parts: the signals to check, and the responsibilities to match.

A LinkedIn profile is a self-authored summary, not evidence of work. Read what the
profile actually says, cite it, and do not infer beyond it.

## Signals

Four signals to check, starting with the required title gate.

### 1. Qualifying title — required gate

The candidate must have held — at some point in their career, not necessarily
their current role — a title from this list:

- Design Engineer
- UX Engineer
- UI Engineer
- Design Technologist

Treat close variants as qualifying (e.g. "Senior Design Engineer", "Product
Design Engineer", "UX Engineer II"). Adjacent but non-qualifying titles — pure
"Product Designer", "Frontend Engineer", "Software Engineer" — do not satisfy
this gate on their own.

If the candidate has never held a qualifying title, the verdict is **Not a fit**.
State this plainly and explain why. Still complete the rest of the evaluation so
the reader has the full picture.

### 2. Career progression — strong bonus, not required

A candidate who held a **product design** role earlier in their career and then
moved into design engineering is a great signal — it shows the design foundation
behind the engineering work. This is a strong positive when present, but its
absence is not a mark against the candidate.

### 3. User-centered language — bonus

Look for language showing a user-centered approach: mentions of users, user
research, user needs, customer workflows, usability. A profile that talks only
about technology and shipping, with no mention of the people the work is for, is
weaker on this signal.

### 4. Design portfolio

A linked design portfolio is ideal — look for one in the profile's headline,
about section, featured section, or contact info. If no portfolio link appears
anywhere on the profile, call `$designer-link-discovery` to search for one before
reporting it as missing.

## Design Engineer Responsibilities

Match the profile against this checklist. For each responsibility, decide whether
the profile shows evidence for it and cite the supporting text. Mark anything with
no supporting evidence as "Not shown" rather than guessing.

- Create high-fidelity, coded prototypes and interaction models that bring
  complex, AI-powered workflows to life and clearly communicate product direction.
- Own high-impact initiatives from concept through production, shipping
  experiences that reflect strong design thinking, technical rigor, and a deep
  understanding of customer workflows.
- Lead technical design discussions, make architectural decisions, and guide
  cross-team implementation efforts.
- Advance the company's design system as a core piece of product infrastructure —
  defining scalable patterns, enabling consistency, and accelerating teams
  building across AI-native experiences.
- Collaborate closely with product managers, engineers, and other stakeholders to
  research and evaluate user requirements, translate them into effective designs,
  and ensure the final product meets both user and business needs.
- Collaborate with engineering teams during development, advocating for design
  quality and user experience by championing design fidelity and attention to
  detail throughout the development lifecycle.
- Drive cross-functional alignment by helping teams navigate tradeoffs between
  technical constraints, business goals, and user experience to deliver
  high-quality outcomes.

## Verdict

Weigh the signals and the responsibilities match into one of three verdicts:

- **Good fit** — has a qualifying title, and the profile shows clear evidence
  across most of the responsibilities.
- **Possible fit** — has a qualifying title, but the responsibilities evidence is
  partial or thin; worth a closer look or a screen to confirm.
- **Not a fit** — no qualifying title was ever held, or the profile shows little
  evidence of the responsibilities.
