# Interview Question Bank

Reference library of follow-up questions for portfolio reviews. Organize questions by the competency they probe. Always customize to the specific portfolio — never ask generic questions when a specific one is available.

## Probing Product & Strategic Impact

- "How did you arrive at this problem definition? Was it given to you, or did you reframe it?"
  - Strong answer: Describes challenging the initial brief, stakeholder alignment, or research that shifted the frame.
- "What did you say no to in this project? How did you narrow the scope?"
  - Strong answer: Shows deliberate scoping with rationale, not just "we ran out of time."
- "If you had to re-do this project, would you frame the problem the same way?"
  - Strong answer: Shows reflective thinking and growth, not just "yes, it went great."
- "You mention [metric] improved. How confident are you that your design caused that change?"
  - Strong answer: Acknowledges confounding factors, describes attribution method (A/B test, before/after with controls).
- "How did you decide what to measure? Were there metrics you considered but didn't track?"
  - Strong answer: Describes metric selection process, trade-offs between metrics, and why some were excluded.

## Probing Customer-Centric Research & Insight

- "I see the final design but not much exploration. Walk me through the ideas you considered and discarded."
  - Strong answer: Describes 2-3 alternatives with specific reasons each was deprioritized.
- "How did you decide when you had enough research to move forward?"
  - Strong answer: Describes a decision framework or threshold, not just "we felt confident."
- "What was the biggest pivot in this project? What caused it?"
  - Strong answer: Describes a specific moment where evidence changed the direction.
- "What didn't go as well as you hoped after launch? What would you improve?"
  - Strong answer: Shows honest self-assessment grounded in post-launch evidence.

## Probing Craft & Experience Quality

- "Walk me through the interaction for [specific component]. What happens on hover, focus, and error?"
  - Strong answer: Describes states, transitions, and edge cases fluently — even if not shown in portfolio.
- "How did you handle [specific responsive scenario] on mobile?"
  - Strong answer: Describes mobile-specific design decisions with rationale.
- "What design system constraints did you work within? Where did you deviate and why?"
  - Strong answer: Shows awareness of system-level impact and deliberate deviation with justification.
- "For an AI-driven surface in your work, how did you communicate uncertainty, sources, or override paths to users?"
  - Strong answer: Describes trust patterns (confidence indicators, citations, undo/override) tied to user decisions.

## Probing Systems & Platform Thinking

- "What edge cases did you design for that aren't shown in the portfolio?"
  - Strong answer: Lists specific cases (empty state, error recovery, permissions, first-time vs. returning) with how they were handled.
- "How does this feature behave when [boundary condition — zero items, 1000 items, offline, slow connection]?"
  - Strong answer: Describes deliberate handling of boundary conditions with rationale.
- "What happens to this design if the team adds [adjacent feature] in 6 months? How extensible is it?"
  - Strong answer: Shows forward-thinking and platform-level design awareness.
- "Where in this experience do you hand off to automation, and where does the human stay in the loop?"
  - Strong answer: Articulates explicit boundaries between AI-driven and human-driven steps with rationale.

## Probing AI Fluency & Automation Leverage

- "Walk me through where AI showed up in your workflow for this project — research, exploration, production, or QA."
  - Strong answer: Specific tools and tasks, with reflection on what AI did well and where the candidate corrected or intervened.
- "Do you have prompts, evaluation checklists, or workflows you reuse across projects? Show me one."
  - Strong answer: Demonstrates repeatable patterns rather than ad hoc usage; can articulate why the prompt is structured that way.
- "Where would you NOT use AI in this project, and why?"
  - Strong answer: Shows judgment about when human design thinking, ethics, or qualitative nuance is required.
- "For an AI feature in your portfolio, how did you evaluate output quality before shipping?"
  - Strong answer: Describes evaluation methods (friction logging, eval sets, user testing of generated outputs) and what failures looked like.
- "(Senior+) Have you established AI standards or workflows others on your team adopted?"
  - Strong answer: Concrete example of a reusable prompt, framework, or QA standard with adoption evidence.

## Probing Ownership, Leadership & Collaboration

- "Tell me about a disagreement with [engineering/PM/stakeholder] on this project. How did you resolve it?"
  - Strong answer: Describes the specific disagreement, both perspectives, and how resolution was reached (not "I convinced them").
- "How did you get feedback on this design before shipping? What changed based on that feedback?"
  - Strong answer: Describes a specific critique or review process with concrete changes that resulted.
- "What did someone else on the team contribute that made this project better?"
  - Strong answer: Generous, specific attribution. Red flag if candidate can't name a contribution.

## Probing Level & Leadership (Integrator+ Only)

- "How did you decide what to work on? Did you choose this project or was it assigned?"
  - Strong answer (Integrator): Shows influence over roadmap or problem selection within a domain.
  - Strong answer (Architect/Steward): Shows multi-team or org-level prioritization and strategic thinking.
- "Did you mentor anyone during this project? How did you help them grow?"
  - Strong answer: Specific example of teaching, pairing, or feedback that led to visible improvement.
- "How did you communicate this work to leadership? What was the decision you were driving?"
  - Strong answer: Shows stakeholder management, not just "I presented it."

## Anti-Patterns: Questions to Avoid

- "Tell me about your design process." → Too generic. Ask about a specific case study instead.
- "What's your favorite project?" → Irrelevant to evaluation. Ask about the weakest project for self-awareness.
- "Why did you use [specific tool]?" → Tool choice rarely matters. Ask about design decisions instead.
- "How would you redesign [our product]?" → Unfair without context. Ask about their portfolio work instead.
- "Where do you see yourself in 5 years?" → Not relevant to design evaluation.
